Pierre Boutilier (1720–1796)

The Huguenot Founder — Your 6× Great-Grandfather

⛵ The ship Speedwell, 1752

🇫🇷 Montbéliard, France → Nova Scotia, 1752

The Refugee Who Founded a Nova Scotia Dynasty

Pierre Boutilier was born in 1720 in Montbéliard, in the easternmost part of France — a Protestant enclave surrounded by Catholic territory. As a Huguenot (French Protestant), Pierre's faith made him a second-class citizen in his own country. The Edict of Fontainebleau of 1685 had revoked their protections, and for decades, France's Protestants endured persecution, forced conversion, and exile.

In 1752, when Britain advertised Nova Scotia as a new home for "Foreign Protestants," Pierre saw his chance. With his wife Marguerite Tanner and their three children, he boarded the ship Speedwell, departing for Halifax on the 4th of July — a date that would prove auspicious for more than one nation that century.

They settled Boutilier's Point at St. Margaret's Bay — a rugged harbour on the Atlantic coast of Nova Scotia that still bears his name today. There, Pierre built a home, raised a family, and founded what would become one of Nova Scotia's oldest and most enduring family communities.

  • Arrived Halifax 4 July 1752 on the Speedwell
  • Settled Boutilier's Point, St. Margaret's Bay — the place still bears his name
  • Neighbour Jean Dauphinee arrived on the same ship — your other 6× Great-Grandfather
  • Buried 1796, St. Paul's Anglican Cemetery, French Village
  • Source: PANS vol. 35, doc. 16

Susan Boutilier (~1819 – after 1881)

The Widow of French Village — Your 3× Great-Grandmother

🏠 French Village, St. Margaret's Bay

🇨🇦 French Village, Nova Scotia — 1819 to 1881

A Widow, Three Sons, and a New Beginning

Susan Boutilier was born around 1819 in French Village — the daughter of Francis Boutilier and Susannah Butler, and the great-granddaughter of Pierre's son Jean George. The French Huguenot heritage ran deep in her blood, tempered by her English mother's Anglican faith.

By the time Susan appears in the 1851 Census, she is listed as "Mrs. Susan Boutilier" — head of household, with three sons all under ten years old, and no husband present. She had been widowed young, her first husband having died between 1848 and 1851. The identity of Husband #1 remains a mystery — one of the last puzzles in this family's story.

Around 1855, Susan — a widow in her mid-thirties — married a young Irish newcomer named John Burchell. He was approximately 24 years old to her 36. It was a practical and perhaps affectionate union: he needed a foothold in the community, and she needed a partner to raise her boys. Together they would have seven more children, including John Jr. — your 2× great-grandfather.

The 1871 Census records Susan as age 52, birthplace Nova Scotia, origin: "French." Even as a Burchell by name, she was Boutilier in blood — a daughter of Huguenot France, the last of the fully French line in your direct ancestry.

  • 1851 Census: widowed head of household, 3 sons under 10
  • Her father Francis Boutilier married Susannah Butler, 4 June 1810 (marriage bond in NS Archives)
  • 1871 Census: Susan Birchill, age 52, origin "French," French Village
  • 1881 Census: Susan Burchell, age 60, still living with son John Jr.
  • Seven children with John Burchell: John Jr., Benjamin, William, James, George, Henry, Eliza

John Burchell (c.1831–1894)

The Irish Immigrant — Your 3× Great-Grandfather

☘️ Ireland to Nova Scotia — the famine years

🇮🇪 Ireland → French Village, Nova Scotia — ~1831 to 1894

The Famine Immigrant Who Became a Nova Scotian

John Burchell was born around 1831, likely in Ulster, Ireland. He was a Protestant Irishman — Church of England — which placed him in a distinct community from the Catholic majority who also fled Ireland during the Great Famine years of 1845–1852.

The circumstances of his departure from Ireland are unknown, but by the mid-1850s he had arrived in the fishing communities of Halifax County, Nova Scotia. He was young — perhaps 24 — when he married Susan Boutilier around 1855. The 12-year age gap between them tells a story: she had security and community roots; he had youth and energy. They built a life at French Village together.

The 1871 Census, which recorded the family as "Birchill" (the enumerator mishearing John's Irish accent), shows a thriving household: John at 40 described as a Fisherman, Susan at 52, and six sons ranging in age from 14 to 1. By 1881, he had become a Farmer — a man who had made himself entirely at home in Nova Scotia.

John died in 1894 at French Village, never having left. An Irishman who became a Nova Scotian, and whose blood runs in Glace Bay to this day.

  • Born ~1831 Ireland; Church of England, Irish origin
  • Arrived Nova Scotia ~1850–1855 during/after the Great Famine
  • Married Susan Boutilier ~1855; she was a widow 12 years his senior
  • 1871 Census: "Birchill, John, 40, Fisherman, Ireland" — French Village
  • 1881 Census: "Burchell, John, 51, Farmer" — same home
  • Died 1894, French Village, Halifax County

John Burchell Jr. (1857–1930)

The Fisherman of French Village — Your 2× Great-Grandfather

⚓ St. Margaret's Bay, Nova Scotia

🇨🇦 French Village, Nova Scotia — 1857 to 1930

Born of Two Worlds — The First True Nova Scotian Burchell

John Burchell Jr. was born around 1857 in French Village, St. Margaret's Bay — the son of Irish immigrant John Burchell and French Huguenot descendant Susan Boutilier. In his blood ran the Atlantic crossings of two peoples: Huguenot refugees from France and famine survivors from Ireland.

He grew up watching his father fish the cold waters of St. Margaret's Bay, and the sea became his own calling. On the 1st of January 1881 — New Year's Day — John Jr. married Eliza Jane Connolly in an Anglican ceremony at St. Margaret's Bay. She was 21, the daughter of John Connolly, a coaster who ran trading ships along the shore. The two families had likely known each other for years — they were neighbours in the same small village.

Their son Ernest arrived ten months later, on November 12, 1881. The 1881 Census found John at 24, a fisherman, living with his new wife and his parents. He remained in French Village his entire life, a true son of the Bay.

  • Born ~1857 French Village — son of Irish father and French Huguenot mother
  • Married Eliza Jane Connolly, 1 January 1881, St. Margaret's Bay (Anglican, by Banns)
  • Father's profession: Fisherman; Eliza's father John Connolly: Coaster
  • Son Ernest born 12 November 1881, St. Margaret's Bay
  • Died 1930; remained in French Village throughout his life
  • Source: NS Archives Marriage Register

Ernest William Burchell (1881–1945)

The Coal Miner of Glace Bay — Your Great-Great-Grandfather

⛏️ St. Margaret's Bay → Glace Bay, Cape Breton — 1881 to 1945

From the Sea to the Mines — The Cape Breton Chapter Begins

Ernest William Burchell was born on November 12, 1881 in St. Margaret's Bay — the son of a fisherman and a fisherwoman's daughter. He inherited the sea, first becoming a Seaman. By 1907, he had made his way to Louisbourg, Cape Breton.

On his 26th birthday — November 12, 1907 — Ernest married Annie McDonald in Sydney, Cape Breton. Annie was 20, born in Louisbourg, the daughter of Alex McDonald, a coal trimmer. Ernest's new family was already embedded in Cape Breton's coal economy.

They settled at 677 Arthur Street, Glace Bay — a town built on coal. For 35 years, Ernest worked as a fireman for the Dominion Coal Company, the industrial engine of the region. The work was dangerous: the mines took men's health as surely as they took their labour. Ernest was diagnosed with Pulmonary Tuberculosis — a disease epidemic among coal workers whose lungs filled with dust.

He died at Glace Bay General Hospital on December 8, 1945, and was buried at Greenwood Cemetery three days later. His son Daniel reported his death. His death certificate — one of the primary documents in this collection — confirms every detail of his remarkable life.

  • Born 12 November 1881, St. Margaret's Bay, Halifax Co.
  • Married Annie McDonald, 12 November 1907, Sydney (his 26th birthday)
  • Lived at 677 Arthur St., Glace Bay — the family home for two generations
  • Fireman, Dominion Coal Company, 35 years
  • Died 8 December 1945, Pulmonary T.B., Glace Bay General Hospital
  • Buried Greenwood Cemetery, December 11, 1945
  • Children: Daniel Burchell, Howard Frederick Burchell

Howard Frederick Burchell (~1922) & Rita Artis Allison

The Glace Bay Generation — Your Great-Grandparents

💍 Glace Bay, Cape Breton — 9 May 1949

"Da Nanny" — The Marriage That Brought Bernard Into the World

Howard Frederick Burchell was born around 1922 in Glace Bay, the son of Ernest and Annie McDonald Burchell. He grew up at 677 Arthur Street — the same address his father listed for decades — in the heart of Cape Breton's coal country.

On the 9th of May, 1949 — four years after his father Ernest's death — Howard married Rita Artis Allison in Glace Bay. This is the marriage certificate affectionately known in the family as "Da Nanny's Marriage Cert" — the document that connects the older generations to the living ones.

From Howard and Rita's union came Marie Elaine Burchell, who married James Taylor (stepfather of Bernard). Their son is Bernard Thomas — the family historian who preserved these records and whose research makes this website possible.

The Burchell name, carried from Ireland to French Village and from St. Margaret's Bay to Glace Bay, lived on in Bernard Thomas. 300 years. Two continents. One unbroken thread.

  • Howard Frederick Burchell, born ~1922 Glace Bay, son of Ernest & Annie
  • Married Rita Artis Allison, 9 May 1949, Glace Bay
  • Lived at 677 Arthur St., Glace Bay — the family home
  • Daughter: Marie Elaine Burchell (m. James Taylor)
  • Grandson: Bernard Thomas — keeper of this story

The Allison Heritage

Scottish Lowlands · Acadian French · Cape Breton Coal Country

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Inverkip, Renfrewshire, Scotland

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 Scotland · ⚜ Acadian Cape Breton · ⛏️ Glace Bay — 1785 to 1949

The Other Side of the Family — Rita Allison's Story

While the Burchell family line traces through French Huguenot refugees and Irish famine immigrants, the Allison line — the family of Rita Artis Allison who married Howard Burchell in 1949 — brings an entirely different but equally rich heritage to the story.

The Allison story begins in Renfrewshire, Scotland — the coastal parishes of Inverkip and Greenock, west of Glasgow. William Allison Sr., born around 1785, married Jean Stewart in Greenock on 10 August 1811. Their son William Jr., born 7 August 1812 in Inverkip, became a sailor. His Atlantic fishing routes eventually brought him to Cape Breton, Nova Scotia, where he met and settled with Louise Fougeau — an Acadian French woman carrying the heritage of France's original Maritime settlers, a people who had survived the Great Deportation of 1755 and rebuilt their communities on Cape Breton's shores.

Their son Francis Allison, born in 1858 in Cape Breton, married Mary Ann Campbell on 21 February 1886 at St. Joseph's Catholic Church, North Sydney. Their son John James Allison, born 21 April 1885 in Sydney Mines, entered Cape Breton's coal country — settling in Glace Bay itself, the same town where Ernest Burchell and his family had planted their roots. In Glace Bay's close-knit coal community, the Allison and Burchell families moved in the same world for two generations before Rita Artis Allison and Howard Frederick Burchell married on 9 May 1949 — joining two great Cape Breton heritage lines forever.

  • William Allison Sr. (~1785) & Jean Stewart married Greenock, Scotland, 10 August 1811
  • William Allison Jr. (1812, Inverkip) — sailor; settled Cape Breton with Acadian French Louise Fougeau
  • Francis Allison (1858) married Mary Ann Campbell, St. Joseph's Catholic Church, North Sydney, 1886
  • John James Allison (1885, Sydney Mines) — coal miner, Glace Bay; married Maria York
  • Rita Artis Allison (~1918, Glace Bay) married Howard Frederick Burchell, 9 May 1949
