Family Timeline

Three centuries of history — key dates from 1720 to the present

Chronology of the Burchell Family

From Montbéliard to Glace Bay — the major events, backed by primary sources

🇫🇷
~1720

Pierre Boutilier Born in France

Pierre Boutilier is born in Montbéliard, eastern France — a Huguenot (French Protestant) in a predominantly Catholic country. His neighbour Jean Dauphinee is born around the same time in the same region.

⛪
1685

Edict of Fontainebleau

Louis XIV revokes legal protections for French Protestants (Huguenots). Decades of persecution follow, driving thousands to flee France. This is the context that would ultimately send Pierre's family across the Atlantic.

⛵
4 July 1752

The Speedwell Arrives in Halifax

Pierre Boutilier (age ~32) and Jean Dauphinee (age ~30) arrive in Halifax, Nova Scotia on the ship Speedwell, among hundreds of "Foreign Protestants" recruited by Britain to settle the colony. Pierre brings wife Marguerite and their 3 children.

Source: PANS vol. 35, doc. 16

⚓
~1752–1755

Settlement at St. Margaret's Bay

Pierre Boutilier settles at Boutilier's Point, St. Margaret's Bay — a location that still bears his family's name today. Jean Dauphinee settles nearby at Hacketts Cove. The two founding families begin building Nova Scotian lives.

🇨🇦
1764

Jean George Boutilier Born in NS

Pierre's son Jean George Boutilier is born in St. Margaret's Bay — the first Nova Scotia-born generation of the Boutilier line. He will later marry Catherine Dauphinee, uniting the two founding families.

💍
~1785

Two Founding Families Unite

Jean George Boutilier marries Catherine Dauphinee — the daughter of Jean Dauphinee, who arrived on the same ship as Pierre. Two Speedwell families, two generations later, become one.

Source: St. Paul's Anglican Parish Register

†
1796

Pierre Boutilier Dies

Pierre Boutilier dies aged approximately 76 and is buried at St. Paul's Anglican Cemetery, French Village, Nova Scotia — having lived to see his family established as one of the Bay's founding families. Jean Dauphinee follows in 1798.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿
~1785

William Allison Sr. Born — Inverkip, Scotland

William Allison Sr. is born in the coastal parish of Inverkip (or Greenock), Renfrewshire, Scotland — a Presbyterian Lowlander in a port town connected to the Atlantic trade. His descendants will eventually settle in Cape Breton, Nova Scotia.

Allison Line — Source: Scotland's People, Old Parish Registers

👶
~1788

Francis Boutilier Born

Francis Boutilier is born in St. Margaret's Bay — son of Jean George and Catherine (Dauphinee) Boutilier. He is your 4× great-grandfather and will be the first of the line whose marriage is documented in a surviving legal record.

📜
4 June 1810

The Marriage Bond

Francis Boutilier files a marriage bond in Halifax County, pledging to marry Susannah Butler. The document — witnessed by Barnard Munro and signed in the presence of Sir George Prevost's administration — is still preserved in the NS Archives. This is the oldest direct documentary evidence in the family's collection.

Source: NS Archives RG 1 v.170 p.78

💍
10 August 1811

William Allison Sr. Marries Jean Stewart — Greenock, Scotland

William Allison Sr. marries Jean Stewart in Greenock Parish, Renfrewshire. Jean is the daughter of James Stewart and Isobel Niven. Their son William Jr. will be born the following year and will eventually cross the Atlantic to Cape Breton.

Allison Line — Source: Scotland's People, Old Parish Registers

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿
7 August 1812

William Allison Jr. Born — Inverkip, Scotland

William Allison Jr. is born in Inverkip, Renfrewshire — son of William Sr. and Jean Stewart. He will grow up near Greenock's docks and become a sailor, following Atlantic fishing routes that lead him to Cape Breton, Nova Scotia.

Allison Line — ancestor of Rita Artis Allison

👶
~1819

Susan Boutilier Born

Susan Boutilier is born in French Village — daughter of Francis and Susannah Butler Boutilier. Her French Huguenot heritage traces back directly to Montbéliard, 1720. Her 1871 census entry would describe her origin as simply "French."

🇮🇪
~1831

John Burchell Born in Ireland

John Burchell is born in Ireland — likely Ulster, a Protestant Irishman. Within two decades, the Great Famine will devastate his homeland and send millions into exile. John's path will lead him to the fishing village of French Village, Nova Scotia.

☘️
1845–1852

The Great Famine — Ireland

The Irish Potato Famine kills over one million people and drives another million to emigrate. John Burchell — likely a young man of 14–21 during these years — survives and eventually makes his way to Nova Scotia, following thousands of other Irish emigrants to British North America.

📋
1851

Susan Widowed — The Census

The 1851 Census of Halifax County records "Mrs. Susan Boutilier" as head of household with three sons all under age 10 — and no husband. Her first husband has died, leaving her a widow in her early thirties. She will not remain alone for long.

Source: 1851 Census Halifax Co., M-3138, Page 8 Line 12

💍
~1855

Susan Boutilier Marries John Burchell

Susan Boutilier (~36) marries John Burchell (~24) — a union that bridges French Huguenot Nova Scotia and Irish immigrant Ireland. The 12-year age gap speaks to practical necessity and mutual benefit. Together they will raise a family in French Village.

��
~1857

John Burchell Jr. Born

John Burchell Jr. is born in French Village — the first child of Susan and John, and the first to bear the Burchell name while carrying both Irish and French Huguenot blood. He will become a fisherman like his father before him.

⚜
1858

Francis Allison Born — Cape Breton, NS

Francis Allison is born in Cape Breton, Nova Scotia — the son of Scottish sailor William Allison Jr. and Acadian French Louise Fougeau. He is the first Allison generation born on Nova Scotian soil, carrying both Scottish and Acadian French heritage.

Allison Line — ancestor of Rita Artis Allison

📋
1871

The Birchill Household

The 1871 Census records "Birchill, John, 40, Fisherman, Ireland" with "Birchill, Susan, 52, French, NS" and six sons at home — the fullest snapshot of the family at their height. The enumerator spelled the name phonetically, capturing John's Irish accent.

Source: 1871 Census Halifax West, Sub-district H, Page 15

💍
1 January 1881

John Jr. Marries Eliza Connolly

John Burchell Jr. (24) marries Eliza Jane Connolly (21) on New Year's Day in an Anglican ceremony at St. Margaret's Bay. Both are from French Village — they likely grew up as neighbours. Their son Ernest arrives just ten months later.

Source: NS Archives Marriage Register

👶
12 November 1881

Ernest William Burchell Born

Ernest William Burchell is born at St. Margaret's Bay — son of John Jr. and Eliza Connolly. He is the future coal miner of Glace Bay, and the bridge between the fishing communities of Halifax County and the industrial coal towns of Cape Breton.

†
1894

John Burchell Sr. Dies — French Village

John Burchell (the Irish immigrant) dies at French Village, Halifax County, at approximately age 63–64. He never left the small bay community where he had arrived as a young stranger. His Irish name was now Nova Scotian.

💍⛏️
12 November 1907

Ernest Marries Annie McDonald — Sydney, CB

Ernest Burchell (26) — a Seaman from Louisbourg — marries Annie McDonald (20) in Sydney, Cape Breton. It is his 26th birthday. The McDonald family are already in coal; her father was a Coal Trimmer. Ernest will follow. The Burchell family's move to Glace Bay has begun.

Source: Marriage register, Sydney, Cape Breton, 1907

⛏️
~1910

Ernest Joins Dominion Coal Company

Ernest Burchell begins work as a fireman for the Dominion Coal Company in Glace Bay. He will work there for 35 years — until his death in 1945. The family settles at 677 Arthur Street, Glace Bay, Cape Breton. The family's Nova Scotian chapter moves from fishing village to coal town.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿
~1918

Rita Artis Allison Born — Glace Bay, Cape Breton

Rita Artis Allison is born in Glace Bay — daughter of John James Allison and Maria York. She carries within her the full sweep of the Allison heritage: Scottish Lowlands, Acadian French, and Cape Breton coal country. She and Howard Burchell will meet and marry in the streets of the same coal town.

Allison Line — "Da Nanny"

👶
~1922

Howard Frederick Burchell Born

Howard Frederick Burchell is born in Glace Bay — son of Ernest and Annie. He grows up in the coal town, at the same 677 Arthur Street address, while his father works the mines of the Dominion Coal Company.

†
8 December 1945

Ernest Burchell Dies — Glace Bay

Ernest William Burchell dies of Pulmonary Tuberculosis at Glace Bay General Hospital, aged 64. He is buried at Greenwood Cemetery on December 11, 1945. His son Daniel reports the death; his son Howard will carry the family name forward.

Source: Death Certificate — primary document in this collection

💍
9 May 1949

Howard Marries Rita Allison — Glace Bay

"Da Nanny's Marriage Cert" — Howard Frederick Burchell marries Rita Artis Allison in Glace Bay, Cape Breton. He still gives 677 Arthur Street as his address. Rita brings with her the Allison heritage: Scottish Lowlands (Renfrewshire, Scotland), Acadian French Cape Breton, and coal-country Nova Scotia. Two families, two heritages, one Glace Bay home.

Source: Marriage Certificate — "DaNannyMarriageCert" in this collection  |  Allison Heritage →

Present Day

Bernard Thomas — Family Historian

Bernard Thomas, the 9th generation from Pierre Boutilier's 1752 arrival in Nova Scotia, pieces together this family story — connecting Irish and French Huguenot heritage, fishing villages and coal towns, into one coherent, documented family history. This website is his tribute to the ancestors who came before.

Places of the Family

The locations that shaped the Burchell heritage

🇫🇷Montbéliard, France

1720–1752 — Birthplace of Pierre Boutilier and Jean Dauphinee. A Protestant enclave in eastern France, bordered by Switzerland and the Holy Roman Empire. The Huguenot heritage of this region drove both men to seek new lives in the New World.

⛵The ship Speedwell

4 July 1752 — The vessel that carried Pierre Boutilier, Jean Dauphinee, and hundreds of other Foreign Protestants from Europe to Halifax, Nova Scotia. The journey across the Atlantic was dangerous and weeks-long. Its arrival changed the demographic history of Nova Scotia.

🏝️Boutilier's Point, St. Margaret's Bay

1752–1907 — Where Pierre Boutilier settled. The point still bears his family name. For five generations, the Boutilier/Burchell line lived and fished along this stunning bay on Nova Scotia's South Shore. French Village — just along the shore — was the family's home.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿Inverkip & Greenock, Scotland

~1785–~1845 — Birthplace of William Allison Sr. (~1785) and his son William Jr. (1812). The coastal parishes of Renfrewshire, west of Glasgow, were Presbyterian Lowland communities connected to the Atlantic through the port of Greenock. William Jr.'s maritime career eventually brought him to Cape Breton, where he married Acadian French Louise Fougeau.

🇮🇪Ireland (Ulster)

~1831–~1850 — John Burchell's homeland. Likely from Ulster province, a Church of England Protestant in a Catholic-majority country wracked by the Great Famine. His exact parish of origin remains unknown — one of the family's open research questions.

⚓French Village, Halifax Co., NS

1752–1907 — The heart of the family's Nova Scotian story. John and Susan Burchell raised seven children here. John Jr. and Eliza Connolly married here. Ernest was born here in 1881. The community is on the Tantallon/French Village coastline of St. Margaret's Bay today.

⛵Louisbourg, Cape Breton

~1905–1907 — Where Ernest Burchell worked as a Seaman before his marriage. Louisbourg, historically one of North America's most fortified cities, was still a working port in the early 20th century. Annie McDonald was also from Louisbourg — where they met.

⛏️Glace Bay, Cape Breton

1907–present — The family's home for the last century. Glace Bay was one of the world's great coal towns, powered by the Dominion Coal Company. 677 Arthur Street was the Burchell family home for at least two generations. Ernest, Howard, and Rita all called Glace Bay home.

🌿Greenwood Cemetery, Glace Bay

1945 — Where Ernest William Burchell was buried on December 11, 1945. The resting place of the first Burchell generation to call Cape Breton home. The cemetery likely holds other members of the extended family as well.
