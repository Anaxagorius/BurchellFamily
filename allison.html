Scottish Origins — Inverkip & Greenock

Renfrewshire, Scotland · Late 18th Century

🏴 Renfrewshire, Scotland

🏴 Inverkip & Greenock, Scotland — c. 1785

The Allison Family of Renfrewshire

The Allison family story begins in the coastal parishes of Renfrewshire, Scotland — a region west of Glasgow where the Clyde estuary opens toward the Atlantic. Inverkip, a rural parish with strong Presbyterian traditions, and the port town of Greenock, with its thriving transatlantic trade, shaped the family that would eventually cross the ocean to Nova Scotia.

William Allison Sr. was born around 1785 in this region. On the 10th of August 1811, he married Jean Stewart in Greenock — a union documented in the Scottish Old Parish Registers. Jean was the daughter of James Stewart and Isobel Niven, placing both families firmly within the Lowland Scottish tradition of careful record-keeping through the Kirk.

Their son William Jr., born on 7 August 1812 in Inverkip, would become the first of the Allison line to cross the Atlantic — drawn by the same maritime world that connected Greenock's docks to the fishing grounds of Atlantic Canada. The Allison surname itself appears in Renfrewshire records from the 1600s, typically associated with farming families and tradesmen — a lineage of quiet endurance and practical Presbyterian faith.

  • William Allison Sr. born ~1785 in the Inverkip / Greenock area, Renfrewshire
  • Married Jean Stewart, 10 August 1811, Greenock, Scotland
  • Jean Stewart: daughter of James Stewart and Isobel Niven
  • William Allison Jr. born 7 August 1812, Inverkip, Renfrewshire
  • Source: Scotland's People, Old Parish Registers, Greenock & Inverkip

A Scottish Sailor Meets Acadian Cape Breton

William Allison Jr. & Louise Fougeau · Mid-19th Century

⛜ Acadian Cape Breton, Nova Scotia

⚓ Scotland → Cape Breton, Nova Scotia — c. 1835–1845

Two Traditions Become One

William Allison Jr. grew up near the docks of Greenock — one of Scotland's busiest Atlantic ports. The seasonal fishing routes that connected the British Isles with Atlantic Canada were a natural path for a young man with the sea in his blood. By the 1830s or 1840s, William's maritime work had brought him to Cape Breton, Nova Scotia.

There he met Louise Fougeau — an Acadian French woman from the Cape Breton region. The Acadians were the original French settlers of Maritime Canada, a people who had endured the Great Deportation of 1755, when British authorities expelled thousands from their homes. Many had returned to Cape Breton and rebuilt their communities along the island's rugged shores, keeping alive their language, their Catholic faith, and their deep connection to the sea and the land.

The union of William Allison and Louise Fougeau brought together the Presbyterian faith of the Scottish Lowlands with the French Catholic maritime tradition of Acadian Nova Scotia — a distinctly Cape Bretonian blending of two Atlantic cultures. Their son Francis, born in 1858 in Cape Breton, was the first Allison fully rooted in Nova Scotian soil: neither wholly Scottish nor wholly French, but a proud child of both worlds.

  • William Allison Jr.: born 7 August 1812, Inverkip, Scotland; sailor and fisherman
  • Louise Fougeau: Acadian French, Cape Breton, Nova Scotia
  • The Fougeau name connects to Acadian French maritime heritage of Cape Breton
  • Son Francis Allison born 1858 in Cape Breton — first Nova Scotia-born generation

Francis Allison — Roots in North Sydney

Cape Breton, Nova Scotia · 1858–1886

⛪ St. Joseph's Catholic Church, North Sydney

🍁 North Sydney, Cape Breton — 1858 to 1886

Marriage at St. Joseph's — A Catholic Cape Breton Wedding

Francis Allison was born in 1858 in Cape Breton, Nova Scotia — the son of Scottish William Jr. and Acadian French Louise Fougeau. Raised in the bilingual, multicultural world of Cape Breton, Francis came of age as the island's coal industry was beginning to transform the region from a collection of fishing villages and farms into one of Canada's great industrial landscapes.

On the 21st of February 1886, Francis married Mary Ann Campbell at St. Joseph's Catholic Church in North Sydney — one of Cape Breton's finest Catholic parishes, whose records provide a firm documentary anchor in the Allison family history. Mary Ann was the daughter of Alexander Campbell and Rachel Young. The Campbell name — widespread throughout Cape Breton and the Scottish Highlands — added yet another strand of the island's rich heritage to the Allison line.

In their household, Scottish, Acadian French, and Cape Breton Catholic traditions mingled daily — a reflection of the island's beautifully complex character.

  • Francis Allison born 1858, Cape Breton, NS — son of William Jr. & Louise Fougeau
  • Married Mary Ann Campbell, 21 February 1886
  • Venue: St. Joseph's Catholic Church, North Sydney, Cape Breton
  • Mary Ann's parents: Alexander Campbell and Rachel Young
  • Source: St. Joseph's Parish Register, North Sydney, CB

John James Allison — Sydney Mines to Glace Bay

The Coal Mining Generation · 1885

⛏ Sydney Mines & Glace Bay, Cape Breton

⛏ Sydney Mines → Glace Bay, Cape Breton — 1885 onward

Born Into Coal Country

John James Allison was born on the 21st of April 1885 in Sydney Mines, Cape Breton — a town whose very name declared its industrial destiny. He entered the world as the coal industry was reshaping Cape Breton from a collection of fishing villages and farms into one of Canada's great coal-producing regions. The Dominion Coal Company was sinking new shafts, building new towns, and drawing men from across the island and beyond.

John James married Maria York, whose English ancestry added another layer to the family's already-complex heritage. Together they settled in Glace Bay — the beating heart of Cape Breton's coal country — where their daughter Rita Artis Allison would be born around 1918.

In the streets of Glace Bay, the Allison family were neighbours and contemporaries of the Burchells. Ernest Burchell, who had arrived in Glace Bay around 1907, worked for the same Dominion Coal Company that shaped the lives of all who lived in that coal-dusted, community-rich town. The two families moved in the same world — separated by a generation — until Howard Burchell and Rita Allison were married on 9 May 1949, bringing two Cape Breton heritage lines together forever.

  • John James Allison born 21 April 1885, Sydney Mines, Cape Breton
  • Parents: Francis Allison and Mary Ann Campbell
  • Married Maria York; settled Glace Bay, Cape Breton
  • Contemporary of the Burchell family in Glace Bay's coal community
  • Daughter: Rita Artis Allison, born ~1918 Glace Bay

Rita Artis Allison — “Da Nanny”

The Union That Brought the Family Together · 9 May 1949

💍 Glace Bay, Cape Breton — 9 May 1949

Two Cape Breton Coal Families Become One

Rita Artis Allison was born around 1918 in Glace Bay — the daughter of John James Allison and Maria York, the granddaughter of Francis and Mary Ann Campbell Allison, and the great-granddaughter of Scottish sailor William Allison Jr. and Acadian French Louise Fougeau. In her blood ran the full sweep of the Allison heritage: Scottish Lowlands, Acadian French Cape Breton, and coal-country Nova Scotia.

On the 9th of May 1949, Rita married Howard Frederick Burchell in Glace Bay. Howard was the son of Ernest Burchell — the coal fireman who had worked for the Dominion Coal Company for 35 years — and the grandson of Irish immigrant John Burchell and French Huguenot descendant Susan Boutilier. Two Atlantic immigrant heritages, meeting in Cape Breton coal country.

The marriage certificate — lovingly known in the family as “Da Nanny's Marriage Cert” — is one of the most cherished documents in this collection. From Howard and Rita came Marie Elaine Burchell, and her son Bernard Thomas — the keeper of this whole magnificent story.

  • Rita Artis Allison: born ~1918 Glace Bay, daughter of John James Allison & Maria York
  • Heritage: Scottish Lowlands · Acadian French · Cape Breton coal country
  • Married Howard Frederick Burchell, 9 May 1949, Glace Bay
  • Daughter: Marie Elaine Burchell (m. James Taylor)
  • Grandson: Bernard Thomas — 9th generation family historian
  • Source: Marriage Certificate — “DaNannyMarriageCert” in this collection

Three Heritages, One Family

🏴 Scottish Lowland Line

The Allison name traces to Renfrewshire — the coastal parishes of Inverkip and the port of Greenock, west of Glasgow. This was Presbyterian Scotland: literate, hardworking, and deeply connected to the Atlantic trade. William Allison Sr. and Jean Stewart planted their family in this tradition in 1811, and their son William Jr. carried it across the ocean to Nova Scotia.

The Allison surname appears in Renfrewshire records from the 1600s, associated with farming families and tradesmen — a lineage of quiet endurance and practical faith that would serve the family well in the coal towns of Cape Breton.

⛜ Acadian French Heritage

Louise Fougeau represented a heritage reaching back to the founding of French Acadie — the colony that shaped Maritime Canada. The Acadians who survived the Great Deportation of 1755 and returned to rebuild in Cape Breton carried with them a language, a Catholic faith, and a relationship with the land and sea unlike any other culture in North America.

Her children and grandchildren would carry this Acadian heritage forward in the bilingual, culturally-rich world of Cape Breton — blending it with Scottish Presbyterian roots in a uniquely Cape Bretonian way.

⛏ Cape Breton Coal Heritage

By the generation of John James Allison (born 1885, Sydney Mines), the family was fully embedded in Cape Breton's industrial coal culture. Sydney Mines and Glace Bay were communities bound by shared labour, shared danger, and shared pride. The Cape Breton identity — Scottish, French, Irish, and everything in between — was forged in those colliery communities.

This was the world Rita Allison was born into, and the world she and Howard Burchell built their family in. It is a heritage worth remembering, and worth celebrating.
