Three Peoples, One Family

🇫🇷 The French Huguenot Line

In 1720, Pierre Boutilier was born in Montbéliard, in eastern France — a region where Protestant faith was a matter of survival. When British Nova Scotia opened its doors to Foreign Protestants in 1752, Pierre and his neighbour Jean Dauphinee loaded their families onto the ship Speedwell and crossed the Atlantic to start anew.

They settled St. Margaret's Bay — a rugged, beautiful stretch of Nova Scotian coastline that would carry the Boutilier name for generations. Their descendants became fishermen, farmers, and community pillars of French Village.

This French Huguenot blood flows directly to Bernard Thomas today.

☘️ The Irish Line

Around 1831, a child was born in Ireland who would one day be known as John Burchell. The Great Famine of the 1840s ravaged Ireland, driving millions from their homeland. John — likely from Ulster, a Protestant Irishman — made his way to Nova Scotia, landing in the fishing communities of Halifax County.

Around 1855, he married Susan Boutilier — a widowed French Huguenot mother of three, twelve years his elder. It was a union of two displaced peoples finding strength in each other. Their son John Jr. was born in 1857, the first of the Burchell line as we know it.

From French Village to the coal mines of Glace Bay, the Irish name Burchell became a Nova Scotian one.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿 The Scottish & Acadian French Line

Around 1785, William Allison Sr. was born in the coastal parish of Inverkip, Renfrewshire, Scotland. His son William Jr., born 1812, became a sailor whose Atlantic routes brought him to Cape Breton — where he married Louise Fougeau, an Acadian French woman carrying the heritage of France's original Maritime settlers.

Their descendants — Francis Allison (1858), John James Allison (1885), and finally Rita Artis Allison (~1918) — were rooted in Cape Breton's coal communities. In 1949, Rita married Howard Frederick Burchell, weaving the Scottish and Acadian French Allison heritage permanently into the Burchell story.

Glace Bay — Where the Family Planted Its Roots

Cape Breton's coal town shaped three generations of Burchells

Ernest William Burchell was born November 12, 1881 in St. Margaret's Bay — the son of a fisherman and a French Huguenot mother. Like many young men of his generation, he heard the call of Cape Breton's booming coal industry. He arrived in Louisbourg as a seaman, married Annie McDonald on his birthday in 1907, and moved to Glace Bay.

For 35 years, Ernest worked as a fireman for the Dominion Coal Company — the industrial giant that powered Cape Breton and Canada. He lived at 677 Arthur Street, Glace Bay, a home his son Howard would also list as his address a generation later. Ernest died on December 8, 1945 of Pulmonary Tuberculosis — a disease devastatingly common among coal workers — and was buried at Greenwood Cemetery three days later.

His son Howard Frederick Burchell carried the family forward. On May 9, 1949, Howard married Rita Artis Allison in Glace Bay — and from that union came Marie Elaine Burchell, and her son Bernard Thomas: the keeper of this family's story.
