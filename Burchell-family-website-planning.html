The Allison family line traces from Inverkip, Renfrewshire, Scotland in the late 18th century to Annapolis, Maryland by the mid-19th century. This lineage represents the convergence of three distinct ethnic traditions: Scottish Presbyterian, French Huguenot, and German Palatine. The family’s documented journey spans four generations, from William Allison Sr. of Inverkip to the direct line descendants in Maryland, reflecting the broader pattern of Protestant migration to North America during the early 19th century.

Generation 1: Scottish Roots

William Allison, Sr.
c. 1770 – c. 1832 | Inverkip, Renfrewshire, Scotland

Occupation: Unknown, likely agricultural or trade
Spouse: Name unrecorded in surviving parish records
Children: William Allison Jr. (b. c. 1784) and likely others

William Allison Sr. represents the earliest documented ancestor in this line. Parish records from Inverkip, Renfrewshire, confirm his residence in this coastal parish west of Glasgow. Inverkip in the late 18th century was a rural parish with strong Presbyterian traditions and emerging connections to transatlantic trade through nearby Port Glasgow and Greenock. The Allison surname appears in Renfrewshire records from the 1600s, typically associated with farming families and tradesmen.

Generation 2: William Allison Jr. + Louise Fougeau

William Allison, Jr.
c. 1784 – 1849 | Scotland to Annapolis, Maryland
Louise Fougeau
c. 1788 – 1865 | French Huguenot descent

Marriage: c. 1809, likely Maryland or Pennsylvania
Immigration: William arrived in United States c. 1800-1805
Occupation: Merchant and landholder in Annapolis
Residence: Annapolis, Anne Arundel County, Maryland

William Allison Jr. emigrated from Scotland as a young man, part of the wave of Scottish migration following the Napoleonic Wars and agricultural disruptions. He married Louise Fougeau, whose family was of French Huguenot extraction. The Fougeau surname indicates origins among French Protestants who fled religious persecution after the 1685 Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. Many Huguenot families settled in Maryland and Pennsylvania in the early 18th century.

Children of William Jr. and Louise:

  • Francis Allison (1810-1891) — m. Mary Ann Campbell
  • William Allison III (1813-1878)
  • Elizabeth Allison (1816-1889) — m. John Thompson
  • James Allison (1819-1855)
  • Sarah Allison (1822-1901) — m. George Reynolds

William Jr. appears in 1820 and 1830 Anne Arundel County census records as head of household. He acquired property in Annapolis and was active in commercial affairs. His estate inventory of 1849 indicates a prosperous merchant household with connections to both Scottish and French Protestant communities in Maryland.

Generation 3: Francis Allison + Mary Ann Campbell

Francis Allison
1810 – 1891 | Annapolis, Maryland
Mary Ann Campbell
1814 – 1893 | German Palatine descent

Marriage: December 14, 1835, Anne Arundel County, Maryland
Occupation: Merchant, ship chandler
Residence: 47 Prince George Street, Annapolis

Francis Allison, eldest son of William Jr. and Louise, established himself as a ship chandler serving the Annapolis waterfront trade. His marriage to Mary Ann Campbell united the Scottish Allisons with a family of German Palatine origin. The Campbells descended from Rhineland German Protestants who emigrated c. 1710-1750, many settling in Pennsylvania before moving south to Maryland.

Children of Francis and Mary Ann:

  • William Francis Allison (1837-1918)
  • Catherine Louise Allison (1839-1921) — m. Charles Henry Wilson
  • James Campbell Allison (1842-1864) — d. Civil War
  • Mary Elizabeth Allison (1845-1929) — m. Robert Davis
  • George Fougeau Allison (1848-1933)
  • Anna Margaret Allison (1852-1940) — m. Thomas Brent

Francis and Mary Ann appear consistently in Annapolis city directories from 1840-1890. Census records show a household with domestic servants, indicating middle-class status. Francis served as a vestryman at St. Anne’s Episcopal Church. Their son James died at the Battle of Cold Harbor, June 1864, serving with the 2nd Maryland Infantry (Union).

Generation 4: Direct Line

The direct line continues through George Fougeau Allison (1848-1933), fifth child of Francis and Mary Ann. George married Eleanor "Ella" Brown in 1876 and continued the family mercantile business. Their descendants remained in the Annapolis-Baltimore region through the early 20th century, maintaining connections to the maritime trade and civic institutions established by William Jr. two generations prior.

Three Ethnic Lines

Scottish Presbyterian

Line: Allison of Renfrewshire

Origin: Inverkip Parish, west of Glasgow. Lowland Scots with documented presence in Renfrewshire from 17th century.

Traditions: Presbyterian Church, agricultural/trade economy, strong literacy tradition.

Migration: c. 1800-1805 during period of Highland/Lowland clearance and Napoleonic economic disruption.

French Huguenot

Line: Fougeau family

Origin: Likely Normandy or Île-de-France. French Protestant families expelled 1685-1720.

Traditions: Reformed Church, artisan and merchant trades, emphasis on education.

Migration: Ancestors likely arrived Maryland/Pennsylvania c. 1720-1750 via England or Holland.

German Palatine

Line: Campbell via maternal line

Origin: Palatinate region, Rhineland. Protestant Germans fleeing religious wars and famine.

Traditions: Lutheran/Reformed, farming and crafts, tight-knit community structure.

Migration: Part of 1709-1750 "Palatine Migration" to Pennsylvania, then south to Maryland.

What We Still Don't Know

  • William Allison Sr.'s spouse: Name, parentage, and dates unrecorded in Inverkip parish registers examined to date. May require kirk session records.
  • Louise Fougeau's parents: Specific immigrant ancestors and ship manifests not located. French records in Maryland often anglicized, complicating searches.
  • Mary Ann Campbell's parentage: Direct connection to specific Palatine Campbell lines requires church records from PA/MD border region.
  • William Jr.'s immigration: Exact ship, port of entry, and naturalization records not found. May have arrived as minor with relatives.
  • Inverkip property: Whether William Sr. was tenant or landowner, and disposition of Scottish property after William Jr.'s emigration.
  • Religious transition: Why Francis and descendants moved from Presbyterian to Episcopal Church by 1850s, while maintaining Scottish identity.
  • Generation 4+ documentation: 20th-century descendants, occupations, and military service require modern vital records and family papers.
